This is just enumerating results, in the order found in Munkres's
/Topology/, and their corresponding formalization in Mizar.

Some notions are implicit in Munkres's book, and Mizar gives us an
explicit definition. To indicate this, I will use "implicit definition"
as the label of the item.

Probably the most important thing missing in Mizar is Urysohn's
metrization theorem.

** \sect12 Topological Spaces
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-e069398f-5504-4884-9001-8e6d9c2de541
:END:

- *Definition.* A {{{dfn(Topology)}}} on a set $X$ is a family of
  subsets $\mathcal{T}$ such that
  1. $X\in\mathcal{T}$ and $\emptyset\in\mathcal{T}$
  2. for any family of subsets of $\mathcal{T}$, their union is also in $\mathcal{T}$
  3. intersections of finitely man elements of $\mathcal{T}$ are also in
     $\mathcal{T}$

  Together, $X$ and a particular topology form a {{{dfn(Topological Space)}}}
  - The idiomatic Mizar formalization begins with defining the data as
    [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#L1][~TopStruct~]] and the axioms as an attribute [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V2][~TopSpace-like~]], then
    bundle them together as a [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#NM1][~TopSpace~]].
  - Remember, for 1-sorted objects ~X~, we write ~[#] X~ for the
    underlying set, and we write ~{} X~ for the empty subset of the
    underlying set.
  - We don't actually need to specify $\emptyset\in\mathcal{T}$ as this
    can be proven ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#T1][~PRE_TOPC:1~]]).
- *Definition.* Given a topological space $(X, \mathcal{T})$ we say a
  subset $U\subset X$ is {{{dfn(Open)}}} if it is a member of the
  topology $U\in\mathcal{T}$
  ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V3][~PRE_TOPC:def 2~]])
- Example: the discrete topology ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tdlat_3.html#V1][~TDLAT_3:def 1~]]) is just the powerset,
  and the ant-discrete topology ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tdlat_3.html#V2][~TDLAT_3:def 2~]]) is just $\{X,\emptyset\}$
- Example: Finite-complement topology doesn't seem to be formalized in Mizar
- *Definition.* Let $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ and $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ be
  topologies on the same set $X$. Then we say $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ is
  {{{dfn(Finer)}}} than $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ if
  $\mathcal{T}_{1}\subset\mathcal{T}_{2}$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/setfam_1.html#R1][~SETFAM_1:def 2~]]) and we say
  $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ is {{{dfn(Coarser)}}} than $\mathcal{T}_{1}$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/setfam_1.html#R2][~SETFAM_1:def 3~]]).

** \sect13 Basis for Topologies
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-8cfbaa13-45c8-4c21-8abc-4617a54bbe0b
:END:

- *Implicit Definition.* Let $X$ be a non-empty set and $A$ be a family
  of subsets of $X$. We call the {{{dfn(Union Closure)}}} of $A$ to be a
  subset family of $X$ denoted $\operatorname{UniCl}(A)$ such that
  $x\in\operatorname{UniCl}(A)$ iff there exists a family $Y$ of subsets
  of $X$ such that $Y\subset A$ and $x\in \bigcup Y$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#K1][~CANTOR_1:def 1~]]).
- *Implicit Definition.* Let $X$ be a non-empty set and $A$ a family of
  subsets of $X$. We call the {{{dfn(Finite-intersection closure)}}} of
  $A$ to be a subset family of $X$ denoted $\operatorname{FinMeetCl}(A)$
  such that $x\in\operatorname{FinMeetCl}(A)$ iff there is a family $Y$
  of subsets of $X$ such that $Y\subset A$ and $Y$ is finite and
  $x\in\bigcap Y$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#K2][~CANTOR_1:def 3~]]).
- *Definition.* Let $X$ be a set. A {{{dfn(Basis)}}} for some topology
  on $X$ is a family $\mathcal{B}$ of subsets of $X$ such that
  1. for each $x\in X$ there exists a $B\in\mathcal{B}$ such that $x\in B$
  2. for each $x\in X$ and $B_{1},B_{2}\in\mathcal{B}$ such that
     $x\in B_{1}\cap B_{2}$, there exists a $B_{3}\in\mathcal{B}$ such that
     $x\in B_{3}$ and $B_{3}\subset B_{1}\cap B_{2}$.
   
  Then the {{{dfn(Topology Generated)}}} by $\mathcal{B}$ is defined as
  the family $\mathcal{T}$ of subsets of $X$ such that for each $U\subset X$ we have
  $U\in\mathcal{T}$ iff for each $x\in X$ such that $x\in U$ there
  exists a basis element $B\in\mathcal{B}$ such that $x\in B\subset U$.
  - Mizar defines the basis for a topological space in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#NM1][~CANTOR_1~]] as a
    family of open sets (which makes it a subset of the topology) such
    that the topology is a subset of its union closure
    ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#V1][~CANTOR_1:def 2~]]) which is a fancy way to say its union closure is
    the topology.
- *Lemma 13.1.* Let $X$ be a set, $\mathcal{B}$ a basis for a topology
  $\mathcal{T}$ on $X$. Then $\mathcal{T}$ is the collection of all
  possible unions of elements of $\mathcal{B}$ (i.e., $\mathcal{T}$ is
  the union closure of $\mathcal{B}$). (This can be taken as the
  defining property of the basis, [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#V1][~CANTOR_1:def 2~]])
- *Lemma 13.2.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a topological space, let
  $\mathcal{C}$ be a collection of subsets of $X$. If for each $x\in X$
  and for each $U\in\mathcal{T}$ such that $x\in U$ there exists a
  $C\in\mathcal{C}$ such that $x\in U\subset C$, then $\mathcal{C}$ is a
  basis for $(X, \mathcal{T})$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Lemma 13.3.* Let $\mathcal{T}_{1}$, $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ be topologies
  on $X$ with bases $\mathcal{B}_{1}$, $\mathcal{B}_{2}$
  respectively. Then $\mathcal{T}_{2}$ is finer than $\mathcal{T}_{1}$
  if and only if for each $x\in X$ and $B_{1}\in\mathcal{B}_{1}$ there
  exists a $B_{2}\in\mathcal{B}_{2}$ such that $x\in B_{2}$ and
  $B_{2}\subset B_{1}$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Definition.* The {{{dfn(Standard Topology)}}} on $\mathbb{R}$ is the
  topology generated by the basis $\mathcal{B}=\{(a,b)\mid a,b\in\mathbb{R}\}$
  given by all open intervals of $\mathbb{R}$.
  - Mizar defines the real numbers equipped with the standard topology
    as the topological space ~R^1~ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/topmetr.html#K3][~TOPMETR:def 6~]]).
- Neither the lower-limit topology (generated by all intervals $[a,b)$)
  nor the K-topology appear to be defined in Mizar. {{{missing()}}}
- *Lemma 13.4.* $\mathbb{R}_{\ell}$ and $\mathbb{R}_{K}$ are finer than
  $\mathbb{R}$, but incomparable to each other.
- *Definition.* A {{{dfn(Subbasis)}}} (or {{{dfn(Prebasis)}}}) $\mathcal{S}$ for a topology on
  $X$ is a collection of subsets of $X$ such that $\bigcup\mathcal{S}=X$
  ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#NM2][~CANTOR_1~]] and [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#V2][~CANTOR_1:def 4~]]).

  The {{{dfn(Topology Generated by a Prebasis)}}} is the collection
  $\mathcal{T}$ of arbitrary unions of finite intersections of subbasis
  elements. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/cantor_1.html#T18][~CANTOR_1:18~]])

** \sect15 The Product Topology on $X\times Y$
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-60708636-c121-425e-8072-e0c766a712ab
:END:

- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T}_{X})$ and $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{Y})$ be
  topological spaces. The {{{dfn(Product Topology)}}} on $X\times Y$ is
  the topology having as its basis the collection $\mathcal{B}$ of all
  sets of the form $U\times V$ where $U\in\mathcal{T}_{X}$ and $V\in\mathcal{T}_{Y}$.
  We call $X\times Y$ equipped with the product topology the
  {{{dfn(Product Space)}}}.
  - The Cartesian product $X\times Y$ of sets in Mizar is ~[:X,Y:]~
  - The product space is defined in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_1.html#K2][~BORSUK_1:def 2~]]
- *Theorem 15.1.* Let $\mathcal{B}$ be a basis for $(X,\mathcal{T}_{X})$
  and $\mathcal{C}$ be a basis for $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{Y})$. Then the
  collection $\mathcal{D}=\{B\times C\mid B\in\mathcal{B},C\in\mathcal{C}\}$
  is a basis for the product topology on $X\times Y$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Definition.* The {{{dfn(Projection Maps)}}} $\pi_{1}\colon X\times Y\to X$
  and $\pi_{2}\colon X\times Y\to Y$ defined by $\pi_{1}(x,y)=x$ and
  $\pi_{2}(x,y)=y$ for $x\in X$ and $y\in Y$.
  - $\pi_{1}$ is ~pr1 (X,Y)~ defined in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/funct_3.html#K7][~FUNCT_3:def 4~]]
  - $\pi_{2}$ is ~pr2 (X,Y)~ in Mizar, defined in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/funct_3.html#K8][~FUNCT_3:def 5~]]
  - The induced projection mappings on the topologies are defined as
    ~Pr1(X,Y)~ and ~Pr2(X,Y)~ in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_1.html#K8][~BORSUK_1:def 4~]] and [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_1.html#K9][~BORSUK_1:def 5~]]
    and its defining property may be found in Theorem [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_1.html#T24][~BORSUK_1:24~]].
- *Theorem 15.2.* The collection $\mathcal{S}=\{\pi_{1}^{-1}(U)\mid U\in\mathcal{T}_{X}\}\cup\{\pi_{2}^{-1}(V)\mid V\in\mathcal{T}_{Y}\}$
  is a prebasis for the product topology on $X\times Y$.
  {{{missing()}}}

** \sect16 The Subspace Topology
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-28b0b9c0-626b-4f31-b897-d5df48d63e0f
:END:

- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a topological space, let
  $Y\subset X$. Then we define the {{{dfn(Subspace Topology)}}} of $Y$
  consists of the collection $\mathcal{T}_{Y}=\{Y\cap U\mid U\in\mathcal{T}\}$
  and it forms a topology on $Y$. We refer to the topological space
  $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{Y})$ as a {{{dfn(Subspace)}}} of $X$.
  - Mizar defines ~SubSpace of T~ in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#M1][~PRE_TOPC:def 4~]].
- *Lemma 16.1.* If $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis for the topology of $X$,
  then $\mathcal{B}_{Y}=\{B\cap Y\mid B\in\mathcal{B}\}$ is a basis for
  the subspace topology of $Y$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Lemma 16.2.* Let $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{\text{sub}})$ be a subspace of $(X,\mathcal{T})$. If $U$ is open in $Y$ and
  $Y$ is open in $X$, then $U$ is open in $X$. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tsep_1.html#T18][~TSEP_1:18~]] proves more
  than this)
- *Theorem 16.3.* If $A$ is a subspace of $X$ and $B$ is a subspace of
  $Y$, then the product topology on $A\times B$ is the same as the
  topology $A\times B$ inherits as a subspace of $X\times Y$.
  ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_3.html#T22][~BORSUK_3:22~]])
  
** \sect17 Closed Sets and Limit Points
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-5b58c44d-594a-4956-9b0d-6cdc39eee2ad
:END:

- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a topological space, let $A$ be
  a subset of $X$. We say $A$ is {{{dfn(Closed)}}} if its complement
  $X\setminus A$ is open. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V4][~PRE_TOPC:def 3~]])
- *Theorem 17.1.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a topological space. Then:
  1. $\emptyset$ is closed (registered in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#T27][~PRE_TOPC~]])
  2. $X$ is closed (registered in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#T12][~PRE_TOPC~]])
  3. Arbitrary intersections of sets are closed ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#T14][~PRE_TOPC:14~]] and [[https://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_2.html#T22][~TOPS_2:22~]])
  4. Finite unions of closed sets are closed ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_2.html#T21][~TOPS_2:21~]])
- *Theorem 17.2.* Let $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{\text{sub}})$ be a subspace of $(X,\mathcal{T})$. Then a set $A$ is closed
  in $Y$ iff there is a closed subset $B\subset X$ such that $A=B\cap Y$.
  {{{missing()}}}
- *Theorem 17.3.* Let $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{\text{sub}})$ be a subspace of $(X,\mathcal{T})$. If $A$ is closed in $Y$
  and $Y$ is closed in $X$, then $A$ is closed in $X$.
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tsep_1.html#T8][~TSEP_1:8~]] proves something more: let $X_{0}$ is a subspace of $X$,
    let $A\subset X$, $C\subset X$, and $B\subset X_{0}$ be $B=A$
    considered as a subset of $X_{0}$. If $C\subset X_{0}$ and $C$ is
    closed in $X$, then $A$ is closed in $X$ iff $B$ is closed in $X_{0}$.
- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a topological space, let $A$ be
  a subset of $X$.
  1. The {{{dfn(Closure)}}} of $A$ is the subset $\operatorname{Cl}(A)$
     of $X$ equal to the intersection of all closed subsets of $X$
     containing $A$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#T16][~PRE_TOPC:16~]] proves the logical equivalence;
     ~TOPS_1~ registers it is a closed subset of $X$.)
     - ~Cl A~ is defined in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#K2][~PRE_TOPC:def 7~]]
  2. The {{{dfn(Interior)}}} of $A$ is the subset
     $\operatorname{Int}(A)$ of $X$ equal to the union of all open
     subsets of $X$ contained in $X$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tex_4.html#T3][~TEX_4:3~]] proves this logical equivalence)
     - ~Int A~ is defined as $X\setminus\operatorname{Cl}(X\setminus A)$ in
       [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_1.html#K1][~TOPS_1:def 1~]]
- *Theorem 17.4.* Let $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{\text{sub}})$ be a subspace of $(X,\mathcal{T})$. Let $A$ be
  a subset of $Y$, $B$ a subset of $X$ such that $A=B$. Then
  $\operatorname{Cl}(A)=\operatorname{Cl}(B)\cap Y$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Theorem 17.5.* Let $A$ be a subset of $(X,\mathcal{T})$.
  1. Then $x\in\operatorname{Cl}(A)$ iff every open subset $U$ of $X$
     containing $x$ intersects $A$ (this is the definition found in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#K2][~PRE_TOPC:def 7~]])
  2. If $\mathcal{B}$ is a basis generating $\mathcal{T}$, then
     $x\in\operatorname{Cl}(A)$ iff every basis element
     $B\in\mathcal{B}$ containing $x\in B$ intersects $A$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Definition.* If $A$ is a subset of $(X,\mathcal{T})$ and if $x\in X$,
  we say $x$ is a {{{dfn(Limit Point)}}} of $A$ (or $x$ is an
  {{{dfn(Accumulation Point)}}} of $A$) if every neighborhood of $x$
  intersects $A$ in some other point than $x$ --- i.e., if $x\in\operatorname{Cl}(A\setminus\{x\})$.
  ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/topgen_1.html#R1][~TOPGEN_1:def 2~]])
- *Implicit Definition.* The {{{dfn(Derivative)}}} of a set $A$ is the
  set of limit points of $A$ and denoted
  $\operatorname{Der}(A)$. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/topgen_1.html#K2][~TOPGEN_1:def 3~]])
- *Theorem 17.6.* Let $A$ be a subset of a topological space
  $(X,\mathcal{T})$. Let $A'$ be the set of limit points of $A$. Then
  $A\cup A'=\operatorname{Cl}(A)$. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/topgen_1.html#T29][~TOPGEN_1:29~]])
- *Corollary 17.7.* A subset of a topological space is closed iff it
  contains all its limit points ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/topgen_4.html#T3][~TOPGEN_4:3~]])
- *Definition.* A topological space $(X,\mathcal{T})$ is called
  {{{dfn(Hausdorff)}}} (or $T_{2}$) if for each distinct pair of points
  $x_{1},x_{2}\in X$ there exists open sets $U_{1}\in\mathcal{T}$
  and $U_{2}\in\mathcal{T}$ such that $x_{1}\in U_{1}$ and $x_{2}\in U_{2}$
  and $U_{1}\cap U_{2}=\emptyset$.
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V8][~PRE_TOPC:def 10~]] defines ~T_2~
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/compts_1.html#NV4][~COMPTS_1~]] introduces ~Hausdorff~ as a synonym for ~T_2~
- *Theorem 17.8.* Every finite point set in a Hausdorff space
  $(X,\mathcal{T})$ is closed.
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pcomps_1.html#T7][~PCOMPS_1:7~]] proves singleton subsets are closed, then we can use
    previous results to establish the claim.
- *Implicit Definition.* The $T_{1}$ property is defined and found in
  [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V7][~PRE_TOPC:def 9~]].
- *Theorem 17.9.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a $T_{1}$ space, let $A$ be a
  subset of $X$. Then a point $x$ is a limit point of $A$ iff every
  neighborhood of $x$ contains infinitely many points of $A$. {{{missing()}}}
- *Theorem 17.10.* If $(X,\mathcal{T})$ is a Hausdorff space, then a
  sequence of points converges to at most one point of $X$.
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/frechet2.html#T24][~FRECHET_2:24~]] proves this
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/frechet.html#V2][~FRECHET2:def 2~]] defined ~convergent~ for a sequence of points
- *Theorem 17.11.*
  1. Every simply ordered set is a Hausdorff space in the order
     topology. {{{missing()}}}
  2. The product of 2 Hausdorff spaces is a Hausdorff space (registered
     in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_2.html#FC7][~BORSUK_2~]])
  3. A subspace of a Hausdorff space is Hausdorff (registered in [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/jordan.html#CC5][~JORDAN~]])

- *Implicit Definition.* Let $(X, \mathcal{T})$ be a topological space
  and $A\subset X$ be a subset. We define the {{{dfn(Boundary)}}} of $A$
  by the equation $\partial A = \operatorname{Cl}(A)\cap\operatorname{Cl}(X\setminus A)$.
  - I think Bourbaki uses the symbol $\operatorname{Fr}(A)$ instead of
    $\partial A$, because the French word for "boundary" is /fronti√®re/.
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_1.html#K2][~TOPS_1:def 2~]] uses the complement $A^{\complement} = X\setminus A$
    in its definition, but is otherwise what we have written down.
  - Many results concerning the boundary may be found in ~TOPS_1~
  
** \sect18 Continuous Functions
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-fa0dd80e-9205-49a1-9a43-d6508966bdd6
:END:

- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T}_{X})$ and $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{Y})$ be
  topological spaces, $f\colon X\to Y$ be a function. We say $f$ is
  {{{dfn(Continuous)}}} if for each $V\in\mathcal{T}_{Y}$, its preimage
  under $f$ is open in $X$, i.e., $f^{-1}(V)\in\mathcal{T}_{X}$
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_2.html#T43][~TOPS_2:43~]] proves this logical equivalence
- *Theorem 18.1.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T}_{X})$ and $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{Y})$ be
  topological space, let $f\colon X\to Y$. Then the following are
  logically equivalence:
  1. $f$ is continuous
  2. For every subset $A$ of $X$, we have
     $f(\operatorname{Cl}(A))\subset\operatorname{Cl}(f(A))$ ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_2.html#T45][~TOPS_2:45~]])
  3. For every subset $B$ of $Y$, we have $f^{-1}(Y)$ be closed in $X$
     ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V5][~PRE_TOPC:def 6~]])
  4. For each $x\in X$ and $V\in\mathcal{T}_{Y}$ containing $f(x)\in V$,
     there is a $U\in\mathcal{T}_{X}$ such that $f(U)\subset V$
     ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tmap_1.html#T43][~TMAP_1:43~]], [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tmap_1.html#T44][~TMAP_1:44~]])
- *Definition.* Let $f\colon X\to Y$ be a map of topological spaces. We
  call $f$ a {{{dfn(Homeomorphism)}}} if $f$ is a bijection and $f$ is
  continuous and $f^{-1}$ is
  continuous. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/tops_2.html#V3][~TOPS_2:def 5~]])

  In this case, we say $X$ and $Y$ {{{dfn(are Homeomorphic)}}}.
  ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/t_0topsp.html#R1][~T_0TOPSP:def 1~]])
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_3.html#R2][~BORSUK_3~]] establishes reflexivity, symmetry of ~are_homeomorphic~
  - [[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/borsuk_3.html#T3][~BORSUK_3:3~]] proves transitivity of ~are_homeomorphic~, establishing
    it is an equivalence relation
- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T}_{X})$ and $(Y,\mathcal{T}_{Y})$ be
  topological space, let $f\colon X\to Y$ be a continuous function. We
  call $f$ an {{{dfn(Embedding)}}} if it is a homeomorphism onto its
  image $f(X)$ considered as a subspace of $Y$ equipped with the
  subspace topology.

- *Definition.* Let $X$ be a set. A {{{dfn(Metric)}}} on $X$ is a
  function $d\colon X\times X\to\mathbb{R}$ satisfying:
  1. $d(x,x)\geq0$ for every $x\in X$
  2. $d(x,y)=d(y,x)$ for every $x,y\in X$
  3. $d(x,y)+d(y,z)\geq d(x,z)$ for every $x,y,z\in X$

  let $x_{0}\in X$. We define the $\varepsilon$ {{{dfn(Ball)}}} centered

- *Definition.* Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a topological space where every
  one-point subset of $X$ is closed.
  1. We call $X$ {{{dfn(Regular)}}} if any point $x_{0}$ and
     closed subset $B\subset X$ not containing $x_{0}\notin B$, there
     exists open sets $U$, $V$ containing $x_{0}\in U$ and $B\subset V$
     which are disjoint $U\cap V=\emptyset$.
     ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V9][~PRE_TOPC:def 11~]])
  2. We call $X$ {{{dfn(Normal)}}} if every pair of disjoint closed
     subsets $A$, $B$ of $X$ are contained in disjoint open subsets of
     $X$.
     ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/pre_topc.html#V10][~PRE_TOPC:def 12~]]).

  one-point subset of $X$ is closed. Then:
  1. $X$ is regular if and only if for every point $x\in X$ and open
     subset $U\subset X$ which contains $x\in U$, there exists a
     $V\subset X$ open such that $x\in V$ and
     $\operatorname{Cl}(V)\subset U$. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/urysohn1.html#T21][~URYSOHN1:21~]])
  2. $X$ is normal if and only if for every subset $A\subset X$ closed
     and every $U\subset X$ open, $A\subset U$ implies there is an open
     $V\subset X$ such that $A\subset V$ and

     ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/connsp_2.html#T20][~CONNSP_2:20~]])
- *Theorem 31.2.* We have the following results:
  1. Subspaces of Hausdorff spaces are Hausdorff. ([[http://mizar.uwb.edu.pl/version/current/html/topmetr.html#T2][~TOPMETR:2~]])

  space,

  {{{missing()}}}

** \sect34 Urysohn Metrization Theorem
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: h-fb013eb5-364a-4a9f-8cff-6228a9b3d538

- *Theorem 34.1* (Urysohn Metrization Theorem). Every regular space with
  a countable basis is metrizable. {{{missing()}}}
- *Theorem 34.2* (Embedding theorem). Let $(X,\mathcal{T})$ be a space
  where all singletons are closed. Suppose $\{f_{\alpha}\}_{\alpha\in J}$ is a
  family of continuous functions $f_{\alpha}\colon X\to\mathbb{R}$
  satisfying the requirement that each point $x_{0}\in X$ and each open
  set $U\subset X$ containing $x_{0}\in U$, there is an index $\alpha\in
  J$ such that $f_{\alpha}$ is positive at $x_{0}$ and vanishes outside
  $U$.

  $F(x)=(f_{\alpha}(x))_{\alpha\in J}$ is an embedding of $X$ in
  $\mathbb{R}^{J}$.

  then $F$ embeds $X$ into $[0,1]^{J}$. {{{missing()}}}

  only if it is homeomorphic to $[0,1]^{J}$ for some $J$. {{{missing()}}}
